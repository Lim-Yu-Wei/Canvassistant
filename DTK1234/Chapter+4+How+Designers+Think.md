# How Designers Think

## Overview
This chapter explores the design philosophies of Gordon Murray and Kenneth Grange, two influential designers from distinct fields—motor racing and industrial design. Despite their different backgrounds, both share a competitive spirit and a commitment to innovation. The chapter highlights their strategic thinking practices and compares their approaches to those of other successful designers, emphasizing the importance of motivation, attitude, and creative thinking in the design process.

## Key Concepts & Definitions
- **Design Thinking**: A methodology that uses the designer's sensibility and methods to match people's needs with what is technologically feasible and what a business can deliver.
- **First Principles**: A foundational approach to problem-solving that involves breaking down complex problems into their most basic elements.
- **Creative Thinking**: The ability to think in new and original ways, often leading to innovative solutions.

## Detailed Technical Breakdown

### Motivation and Attitude
- **Gordon Murray**:
  - Strong competitive drive: "All you want to do is go back next time and blow them all into the weeds."
  - Personal involvement in design: Designed and built his own sports car.
  - Innovative layout concepts: Developed the three-seat layout for the McLaren F1.
  
- **Kenneth Grange**:
  - Learning by doing: Gained practical skills by disassembling artillery mechanisms.
  - Physical health as a resource: Believes physical strength contributes to leadership and team success.
  - "I can do it" attitude: Emphasizes a proactive approach to challenges.

### Design Process
- **Gordon Murray**:
  - Works from first principles: Surprised by others' reliance on copying successful designs.
  - Unstructured personal design process: Utilizes annotated sketches rather than formal plans.

- **Kenneth Grange**:
  - Goes beyond the brief: Encourages unexpected outcomes rather than strict adherence to specifications.
  - Emphasizes perceptual insight: Finds solutions through a fresh perspective on design problems.

### Common Practices Among Designers
- **Intense Activity and Reflection**: Successful designers balance periods of focused work with reflective contemplation.
- **Use of Drawing**: Drawing serves as a critical tool for thinking and discovery in the design process.

> [!note] **Key Insight**: Both Murray and Grange exemplify how personal motivation and a willingness to take risks are crucial for innovation in design.
> 
> [!important] **Takeaway**: Successful designers often share a commitment to continuous learning and adaptation, which allows them to push boundaries and achieve remarkable results.
